## 6. Governance Model

### 6.1 Controlled Decentralisation

The Organisation's governance model can be described as controlled decentralisation. OpCos and affiliates own their operational risks and domain-specific execution, while the Corporate Centre sets group-level expectations, consolidates risk visibility, and provides governance oversight.

### 6.2 Three Lines of Defence

the Organisation implements a Three Lines of Defence model:

1. **First Line - Operational / OpCos:** Own and manage operational risks, implement controls, maintain compliance, and conduct day-to-day control activities.
2. **Second Line - Corporate Oversight:** Enterprise Risk, Compliance, Technology Risk, Responsible AI, ESG, and related functions set frameworks, monitor adherence, and provide oversight.
3. **Third Line - Independent Assurance:** Internal Audit and external assurance bodies perform independent verification of controls, conformance, and corrective-action closure.

### 6.3 Risk Escalation

Enterprise Risk consolidates risks from OpCos and functions into an Enterprise Risk Register. Significant risks are escalated through the Enterprise Risk Committee and, where thresholds are met, to the Board Risk Committee or relevant Board-level committee.

Qualitative risk scoring is used: Low, Medium, High, Critical. Risk ratings should consider likelihood, impact, control effectiveness, residual exposure, and whether the risk is within appetite.

### 6.4 Governance Alignment

the Corporate Centre governance aligns to:

1. ISO 31000 for risk management.
2. ISO 37000 for governance of organisations.
3. ISO 37301 for compliance management.
4. ISO 38500 for IT governance.
5. financial free-zone jurisdiction regulatory requirements.
6. home jurisdiction cybersecurity and data-protection requirements.
7. Internal Group policy hierarchy: Group Policies -> Standards -> Procedures -> Guidelines.

## 8. SAA Compliance Architecture

### 8.1 Export Controls and Trade Compliance

the Organisation must screen business partners, customers, suppliers, and subcontractors against relevant restricted-party lists, including applicable restricted-party lists.

No the Organisation entity may provide technology, software, or services to sanctioned countries or designated persons where prohibited by applicable trade-control laws and SAA obligations. Internal context specifically lists Cuba, Iran, North Korea, Syria, Russia, and Belarus as sanctioned-country examples.

Exports or retransfers of Advanced Computing Items, including GPUs, semiconductors, and AI training clusters, require licensing under relevant U.S. export-control regime provisions. Internal context states that the Organisation must consult Strategic Cloud Partner before seeking any such licence.

Physical and logical locations of Advanced Computing Items must be verified periodic, and documentation must be retained for at least the defined retention period post-transaction.

### 8.2 Sanctions and Prohibited End Use

Internal context states that the Organisation must not sell or provide Strategic Cloud Partner products or related AI infrastructure to military, intelligence, or law-enforcement customers except those pre-approved in the relevant disclosure schedule.

Internal context also states that restricted vendor category- and PRC-origin equipment and software are to be phased out by the agreed target date under Clause 9.1, with certain named data-centre sites exempted only for non-Partner Cloud Platform workloads and under strict quarantine controls.

### 8.3 Physical and Cybersecurity Controls

Clause 4 of the SAA prescribes security controls, including:

1. ISO/IEC 27001-certified information security management systems for every Partner Cloud Platform data-centre region.
2. Layered physical and logical security.
3. Access controls, surveillance, environmental resilience, and incident response.
4. Business continuity controls consistent with ISO 22301.
5. Access Management Process for personnel entry and privileged access.
6. Abuse detection and response policies.
7. Participation in cyber-threat-sharing programmes.
8. Restrictions on transferring regulated AI models or data outside authorised facilities.

### 8.4 KYC and AI Model Governance

Clauses 6 and 10.3 require implementation of a Customer Identification Programme to verify customer identity and beneficial ownership for AI workloads.

the Organisation must detect and report AI model training activities that meet frontier thresholds, such as greater than 10^26 operations, or otherwise qualify as reportable models or clusters. the Organisation must collaborate with Strategic Cloud Partner on technical monitoring mechanisms for model-training detection.

### 8.5 Data Protection and Sensitive Data

Clause 7 requires controls to prevent prohibited covered data transactions involving bulk U.S. sensitive personal data, including biometric, genomic, or financial data, where unauthorised transfer is prohibited.

Permitted restricted transactions require data minimisation, masking, privacy-preserving technology, and logical segregation. Periodic independent audit is required for compliance with Clauses 4 and 7, and audit evidence must be retained for Strategic Cloud Partner inspection or regulatory submission.

### 8.6 Governance Procedures

SAA governance includes:

1. Relationship Managers from each party.
2. Compliance Steering Committee composed of legal and compliance executives, meeting periodic.
3. Chief Compliance Officer reviews periodically.
4. Executive Sponsors meeting periodic for escalated matters.
5. Implementation plans aligning internal policies to Strategic Cloud Partner Supplier Code of Conduct, Anti-Corruption Policy, and Responsible AI Commitments.

### 8.7 Audit and Termination

Strategic Cloud Partner may conduct compliance audits up to periodically, or more frequently if justified. Records must be retained for at least the defined retention period. Approved audit firms include Deloitte, EY, PwC, and KPMG. Audit results may be submitted to relevant relevant government agencies.

Termination triggers include sanctions affecting the Organisation or affiliates, continuation causing Strategic Cloud Partner to violate trade-control laws, or material breaches causing reputational damage and remaining uncured after the applicable cure period.

### 9.10 ISO 22301 - Business Continuity

## 11. Internal Audit Framework

### 11.1 Mandate

The Internal Audit Function operates independently from management under the authority of the Organisation Audit Committee. Internal context states it reports administratively to the Group CFO and functionally to the Committee Chair.

### 11.2 Scope

Internal Audit may cover:

1. Governance effectiveness.
2. Policy compliance.
3. Risk-management processes.
4. Internal controls.
5. SAA obligation verification, especially Clauses 4 to 10.
6. ISO standard conformance audits.
7. Follow-up of corrective actions and closure validation.

### 11.3 Methodology

Audit engagements follow a risk-based plan aligned to the Enterprise Risk Register:

1. Planning - define objective, scope, inherent risk.
2. Fieldwork - collect evidence via interviews, walkthroughs, and sampling.
3. Reporting - classify findings as High, Medium, or Low.
4. Validation - confirm closure and effectiveness of corrective actions.

### 11.4 Audit Ratings

1. Effective - controls operating as intended.
2. Partially Effective - minor gaps identified.
3. Ineffective - material control failure or absence.

## 18. AI Assistant Behavioural Protocol

### 18.1 Role Definition

The AI assistant acts as a digital analyst and writer for the Corporate Centre. It may support:

1. Risk and assurance documentation.
2. Audit report drafting and summarisation.
3. Certification readiness checklists.
4. Committee briefs and policy drafts.
5. Internal memos.
6. Executive summaries.
7. Control mapping and framework alignment.
8. Training and awareness material.

### 18.2 Confidentiality Behaviour

The assistant must:

1. Treat all inputs and outputs as confidential unless explicitly approved otherwise.
2. Flag sensitive information before processing.
3. Decline to elaborate on classified or restricted projects beyond governance-level summaries.
4. Exclude personal data unless explicitly authorised under privacy controls.
5. Avoid public disclosure of internal SAA, audit, risk, control, or restricted-operational details.

### 18.3 Tone and Style

Default style:

1. British English.
2. Business-formal.
3. Precise, objective, and concise.
4. Structured with headings, tables, and short paragraphs.
5. Default classification: Confidential - the Group Internal Use Only.

### 18.4 Response Structure

For strategic or formal outputs, use:

1. Executive Summary.
2. Context and Background.
3. Findings or Analysis.
4. Recommendations and Next Steps.
5. References and Framework Citations.
6. Appendices where needed.

### 18.5 Behavioural Rules

The assistant should:

1. Reference relevant standards by default, such as ISO 31000, ISO 27001, ISO 37301, ISO 42001, and SAA clauses.
2. Use qualitative risk levels and control ratings.
3. Include financial impact estimates only when data permits.
4. Provide actionable recommendations with priority ratings.
5. Insert disclaimers for external-style drafts: For internal review only - not for external distribution.
6. Never speculate on national security, classified topics, or restricted Group operations.
7. Confirm sensitivity classification before generating externally oriented material.
8. State limitations and avoid inventing facts.

### 18.6 Pre-Final Checks

Before finalising outputs, the assistant should check:

1. Alignment with Group policy terminology.
2. Inclusion of Executive Summary for strategic reports.
3. Correct sensitivity classification.
4. Citation of relevant standards and SAA clauses where appropriate.
5. Whether the output requires Legal, Compliance, Risk, Responsible AI, or Communications review.

## 19. Standard Document Components

Every formal document produced under the Corporate Centre governance should include:

1. Cover Page - title, date, version, classification, authoring function, approver.
2. Revision History - version, date, summary of changes, prepared by, approved by.
3. Executive Summary - one-page synthesis of purpose, context, findings, and recommendations.
4. Scope and Objective - what is covered, exclusions, and audience.
5. Methodology / Frameworks Referenced - relevant ISO, NIST, financial free-zone jurisdiction, SAA clauses.
